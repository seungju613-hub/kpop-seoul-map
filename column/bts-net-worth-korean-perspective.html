BTS Net Worth — How They Handle Money in Korea

How much money does BTS actually have?

It's one of the most searched questions about them — and the honest answer is: nobody knows for certain. Not even close. The only people who know the real numbers are the members themselves and whoever manages their finances.

So everything in this article is based on estimates — drawn from their publicly known activities, general industry standards, reported real estate transactions, and publicly available stock holdings. Take every number with a grain of salt. But even the estimates paint a fascinating picture — not just of wealth, but of how BTS chooses to handle it.

Estimated Net Worth

BTS's wealth has grown dramatically over the past five years. When "Dynamite" hit #1 on the Billboard Hot 100, their global status — and earning power — reached an entirely different level. HYBE, their parent company, grew at an extraordinary pace alongside them.

Before 2022, BTS rarely did individual activities. Almost everything was group work, and group earnings were split equally seven ways.

Breaking down BTS's concert and activity earnings.
RM discussing songwriting royalties — a significant additional income source.

The exception: songwriting royalties. Members who write and produce music receive royalties separately. This means RM, SUGA, and j-hope — who have been deeply involved in composing and writing since the beginning — likely earned significantly more than the others in earlier years.

Starting around 2022, as members began enlisting for military service, they also launched solo careers. Solo album revenue goes to the individual member. Members who also did solo tours — Jin, SUGA, and j-hope — would have earned substantial additional income from those.

V in a Coca-Cola advertisement — brand deals add another income stream.

Brand endorsement deals also go to individual members. Each member currently serves as an ambassador for various brands — V for Coca-Cola and TIRTIR, Jimin for Dior, among others.

The HYBE Stock Factor:

When HYBE went public on the Korean stock exchange, each BTS member received approximately 68,000 shares of company stock.

News coverage of BTS members receiving HYBE shares — an unprecedented move in K-pop.

This was highly unusual. It's almost unheard of for idol group members to receive stock in their management company. Bang Si-hyuk acknowledged that BTS was largely responsible for HYBE's success and made the exceptional decision to distribute shares to them.

All seven members initially held onto their shares without selling. After about a year, j-hope, Jin, and RM sold portions of their holdings. But SUGA, V, Jimin, and Jungkook have not sold a single share to this day — meaning each of them still holds HYBE stock worth at minimum approximately ₩20 billion (roughly $15 million).

Their Approach to Money Is Very Un-Korean

Public information about BTS members' apartment purchases.

Here's where things get genuinely interesting — and where BTS diverges sharply from typical Korean behavior.

First, you need to understand Korean real estate culture.

Koreans are obsessed with real estate. This isn't limited to celebrities — it's a national preoccupation. Owning your own home carries enormous cultural weight in Korea. It's not just about having a place to live; it's a marker of social status, financial stability, and personal achievement.

More importantly, real estate is Korea's primary investment vehicle. Property values in Seoul have historically risen dramatically. Ordinary Koreans routinely take on billions of won in debt to purchase apartments, betting that the property will appreciate. Borrowing heavily to buy property isn't seen as reckless — it's considered the smart, responsible thing to do.

A Korean real estate YouTube channel analyzing V's apartment complex.

For wealthy Koreans and celebrities, the expectation is even higher. If you have significant cash and only own one property, people think you're wasting your money. Top Korean celebrities typically buy multiple apartments, entire buildings, or commercial real estate.

When BTS members buy property, it becomes front-page news. Korea has strict transparency requirements for real estate transactions. (We wrote about their neighborhoods in detail here.)

RM showing his home — a rare glimpse inside.

So what did BTS actually do with their money?

Unlike most Korean celebrities — and unlike most wealthy Koreans in general — BTS members have not bought buildings. They have not accumulated multiple investment properties. Each member has simply purchased one home to live in. Some also bought homes for their parents, officially registered as gifts (which means they paid the required gift taxes — everything above board).

They also reportedly paid in full cash for every purchase. In Korean real estate news, every article about a BTS member buying property includes the same notable detail: "He paid the full amount in cash." In a country where leveraging debt for real estate is the norm, paying outright is genuinely unusual.

And then there are the members who haven't touched their HYBE stock at all. SUGA, V, Jimin, and Jungkook have held every single share since they were granted — not trading, not selling, just holding.

Why?

Of course, they're human. It would be strange if they had zero interest in growing their wealth. But BTS's approach to investment is notably conservative — especially by Korean celebrity standards.

Some of this might simply be personal preference. But there's another theory that many Korean fans find compelling:

"If BTS members started actively investing — buying buildings, flipping properties, trading stocks aggressively — every single transaction would be reported by the media. And people would start speculating: Are they having financial problems? Is this investment excessive? Are they evading taxes?

They're cutting off that speculation entirely. None of the seven members want unnecessary controversy to damage BTS's reputation. And more than building wealth, what they prioritize is being able to make music together for a long time — happily, without distractions."

Some might say that sounds too idealistic. But that's BTS.

Seven men who could buy buildings and play the market like every other Korean celebrity — and instead choose to live in one home, hold their stock quietly, and focus on what they actually care about. Their non-religious, self-determined worldview seems to extend to how they handle money too. 💜
